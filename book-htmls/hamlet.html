The House of Denmark

Act I · The Ghost

Elsinore Castle · A kingdom in mourning

Denmark is rotting. Two months since King Hamlet died — and already his widow Gertrude has married his brother Claudius, who now sits the throne. On the battlements of Elsinore, guards have seen something terrible: a ghost in the armour of the dead king. They summon Horatio, the prince's levelheaded friend, to witness it for themselves.

The Ghost walks. It will not speak to the guards. The men agree: they must tell Prince Hamlet.

At court, Hamlet is already in revolt — not yet against Claudius, but against everything. His father is dead; his mother has remarried with obscene speed; and the court around him celebrates while he still wears black. Claudius publicly calls Hamlet's mourning excessive. Hamlet is commanded not to return to university. He is trapped.

"Frailty, thy name is woman!"

— Hamlet, Act I scene ii · in private soliloquy on his mother's remarriage

That night on the battlements, the Ghost appears to Hamlet. It speaks. It is his father's spirit — or claims to be. And it tells him something that changes everything: Claudius did not merely step over a dead man's throne. He poured poison into the sleeping king's ear and murdered him. Denmark's new king is an assassin. Hamlet is commanded to revenge.

"Something is rotten in the state of Denmark."

— Marcellus, Act I scene iv · on the battlements after the Ghost appears
The trap of the command

The Ghost's instruction — "revenge his foul and most unnatural murder" — sounds simple. But Hamlet cannot act on a ghost's word alone. What if it is a devil in disguise, exploiting his grief? The play's great engine starts here: not the Ghost's revelation, but Hamlet's inability to simply believe it.

Act II · Feigned Madness

The court watches · The prince watches back

Hamlet has sworn his friends to silence and adopted a strategy: he will feign madness. If he seems unhinged, he can probe Claudius without triggering suspicion, and perhaps catch the king's reaction to the Ghost's accusation. But the mask is hard to hold — and harder to see through from the outside.

Ophelia reports that Hamlet came to her dishevelled and wild-eyed, staring as if he had escaped from hell. Her father Polonius, the court's chief busybody, has a ready explanation: Hamlet is mad with love for Ophelia, whom Polonius has forbidden to see him. Polonius brings the theory straight to Claudius and Gertrude, who are already worried.

Claudius and Gertrude summon Rosencrantz and Guildenstern — old friends of Hamlet's — and ask them to spy on the prince. Hamlet sees through them almost immediately, with a kind of weary contempt. He is not deceived. He is never deceived.

"What a piece of work is a man! How noble in reason, how infinite in faculty..."

— Hamlet, Act II scene ii · to Rosencrantz and Guildenstern on human greatness and dust

A theatre troupe arrives at Elsinore. Hamlet seizes on the idea: he will have the players perform a scene that mirrors the Ghost's account of the murder. If Claudius flinches, the Ghost told the truth. If not — Hamlet will know the apparition lied. He calls it "The Mousetrap."

Thinking as delay

By Act II, Hamlet has had the Ghost's accusation for weeks and done nothing. The theatre plan is not cowardice — it is philosophy. Hamlet needs proof. But the more he thinks, the more reasons he finds to think further. This is the first sign that thought itself has become his enemy.

Act III · The Play Within the Play

The turning point · Proof and its consequences

Before the play, Hamlet delivers his most famous meditation — weighing the misery of life against the terror of the unknown afterlife. But he cannot act on that despair either.

"To be, or not to be, that is the question."

— Hamlet, Act III scene i · in soliloquy on existence, endurance, and death

In this same scene, Hamlet encounters Ophelia, and turns savagely on her — "Get thee to a nunnery!" — whether from genuine cruelty, performed madness, or a suspicion that she is being used to spy on him. Ophelia is devastated. Whatever Hamlet felt for her, it looks now like ruin.

The play is performed. The players re-enact the murder as the Ghost described it. Claudius watches — and stands, and calls for light, and storms out. The proof Hamlet needed is now in his hands.

"The lady doth protest too much, methinks."

— Gertrude, Act III scene ii · watching the Player Queen's excessive oaths of fidelity

Now Hamlet has proof. And he still does not act. He finds Claudius alone, kneeling in prayer — the perfect moment for revenge. But he stops himself: killing a man at prayer might send him to heaven. Hamlet wants damnation for Claudius, not absolution. He waits for a worse moment, and loses his only clean chance.

Sent to confront his mother in her chamber, Hamlet delivers a scalding attack on her remarriage. A noise from behind the arras — Polonius is hidden there, spying. Hamlet drives his sword through the curtain without looking. Polonius falls dead. "I took thee for thy better," Hamlet says. He is wrong.

The cost of delay

The killing of Polonius is the play's hinge. It is not the revenge Hamlet was charged with; it is a blind, impulsive act after weeks of paralysed deliberation. Everything that follows — Ophelia's madness, Laertes' vendetta, Hamlet's exile — flows from this one moment of misplaced urgency.

Act IV · Exile and Grief

The court unravels · Madness becomes real

Claudius moves quickly. Polonius's killing must be managed — it cannot be punished openly without revealing what Hamlet knows. So Claudius packs Hamlet off to England with Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, carrying sealed letters to the English king: orders for Hamlet's execution. The prince, still alive, still reasoning, is being sent to die by the man he has not yet managed to kill.

Meanwhile, Ophelia has broken. Her father is dead; her lover was his killer and is now gone. She appears at court distributing flowers, singing fragments of songs, her speech scattered and strange. This is not performance. This is true madness — the thing Hamlet only mimicked. The court watches her and does not know what to do.

From France, Laertes storms back to Elsinore with a mob behind him, demanding justice for his father's murder. Claudius, the master politician, turns Laertes' fury precisely where he wants it: not at himself, the real author of the court's corruption, but at Hamlet. Laertes will be Claudius's instrument of revenge.

"Too much of water hast thou, poor Ophelia, and therefore I forbid my tears."

— Laertes, Act IV scene vii · on hearing of his sister's drowning

Ophelia drowns. Garlands, a willow branch, water weeds — she slips beneath the brook's surface, singing as she goes. Whether she intended it no one says. Gertrude delivers the news with terrible beauty. Laertes' grief converts instantly to a harder, colder substance.

On the ship to England, Hamlet has read the sealed orders. He forged new letters, redirecting the execution orders to Rosencrantz and Guildenstern. A pirate attack allowed him to return to Denmark alone. He writes to Horatio: he is coming back.

Two kinds of madness

Shakespeare places Ophelia's real collapse against Hamlet's feigned disorder deliberately. Ophelia has no power to act, no philosophy to retreat into — only grief, which destroys her. Hamlet has all the tools of reason and still cannot move. The play asks which of them is more truly trapped.

Act V · The Reckoning

The graveyard and the court · All debts paid

Hamlet returns to Denmark, and the first thing he encounters is a graveyard. Two gravediggers are digging Ophelia's grave, joking about death with the indifference of men who live beside it. Hamlet picks up a skull — it turns out to be Yorick, the court jester who carried him on his shoulders as a boy. Here is where all wit and flesh end.

"Alas, poor Yorick! I knew him, Horatio."

— Hamlet, Act V scene i · holding Yorick's skull in the graveyard

Ophelia's funeral procession arrives. Laertes leaps into the grave, mad with grief. Hamlet reveals himself and they grapple. Hamlet insists his love for Ophelia was real — whatever his cruelty appeared. No one is persuaded. The damage is done.

Claudius now accelerates his plan. He and Laertes have arranged a fencing match — Laertes will use an unbated, poisoned blade. As insurance, Claudius prepares a poisoned cup for Hamlet. The trap is set.

Before the match, Hamlet tells Horatio he has a strange readiness: "the readiness is all." He cannot will the moment of revenge to come. He can only be present when it arrives. Something like acceptance has replaced paralysis — not certainty, but the decision to stop requiring certainty.

"The readiness is all."

— Hamlet, Act V scene ii · to Horatio before the fatal fencing match

The fencing match: Laertes wounds Hamlet with the poisoned blade. In the scuffle the rapiers are exchanged — Hamlet wounds Laertes in turn. Gertrude, drinking to Hamlet's health, takes the poisoned cup. She falls. Laertes, dying, confesses the plot. Hamlet finally acts: he stabs Claudius with the poisoned sword and forces the poisoned wine down his throat. Claudius dies. Hamlet dies. Laertes dies. Gertrude is already gone.

Into this hall of the dead walks Fortinbras, returning victorious from Poland — the man who acted where Hamlet deliberated, who took what he wanted while Hamlet thought about what he should want. He orders soldiers' honours for Hamlet, the soldier-scholar prince who never quite believed in soldiering.

The deaths of everyone

Hamlet is unusual among Shakespeare's tragedies in that virtually everyone dies — not one figure escapes the court's corruption. The final stage is not moral triumph; it is exhaustion. Denmark passes to Fortinbras, the outsider, precisely because the insiders consumed one another. The revenge is complete. The cost was everything.

✦ Why It Matters

Legacy, themes, and the play's enduring force

The central argument
  • Hamlet maps the catastrophic gap between thought and action — a condition that feels eternal because it is internal, not circumstantial.
  • The play asks whether moral certainty is ever achievable, or whether all action is finally a leap into the unknown.
  • It dramatises grief as a destabilising force that reshapes not just emotion but perception — Hamlet cannot trust what he sees.
  • Its characters are not archetypes but psychological portraits; Claudius, Gertrude, Polonius all contain more than their roles suggest.

Revenge and Justice

Hamlet is charged with revenge but haunted by its illegitimacy. Private vengeance is not justice — it is one more killing. He cannot make the two things coincide, and neither can anyone watching.

Performance and Reality

Nearly every character in the play performs: Hamlet feigns madness, Claudius performs grief, Polonius performs wisdom. The theatre-within-the-play makes the theme literal. How does anyone know what is true?

Death and Delay

The "To be or not to be" soliloquy is not about suicide: it is about the terror of action, since every act is irreversible, and death is the most irreversible act of all. Inaction is Hamlet's attempt to stay before the point of no return.

Women and Power

Gertrude and Ophelia operate entirely within what men allow them. Gertrude's choices are constrained by patriarchy and survival; Ophelia has no choices at all. Their fates are not incidental — they are the collateral of male obsession.

Three most famous passages

"To be, or not to be, that is the question: whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, or to take arms against a sea of troubles..."

The most recognisable opening of any soliloquy in world literature — not because it is about suicide, but because it articulates the universal condition of paralysis before an impossible choice.

"What a piece of work is a man! How noble in reason, how infinite in faculty... And yet, to me, what is this quintessence of dust?"

Shakespeare's fullest expression of Renaissance humanism — and its simultaneous collapse. The speech moves from wonder to desolation in four sentences.

"The rest is silence."

Hamlet's last words — five syllables that end four hours of speech, thought, and verbal brilliance. The most compressed exit line in dramatic literature.
