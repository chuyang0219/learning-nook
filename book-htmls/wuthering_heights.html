Wuthering Heights

Emily Brontë · 1847

Character Map

Three generations of the Earnshaw and Linton families, and the narrators who tell their story. Generations run top to bottom; lines show blood, marriage, and the bonds that shape the plot.

How to read this The Earnshaw line (left) and Linton line (right) are joined by Catherine's marriage to Edgar — and invaded by Heathcliff, who enters as a foundling and methodically takes possession of both estates. The third generation re-enacts the first, but with a chance for the cycle to break. Nelly Dean and Lockwood are outside the family web entirely: they are the lenses, not the subjects.

Chapter I · The Stranger Arrives

The year is 1801. Lockwood, a self-styled misanthrope newly arrived from London, has taken the lease on Thrushcross Grange — a comfortable manor on the Yorkshire moors. Seeking out his landlord, he makes his way across the windswept heath to Wuthering Heights, a farmhouse as forbidding as its name suggests: the very architecture seems built against weather and human warmth alike.

He is received with surly hostility by Heathcliff, a dark, weathered man of obscure origins who seems to gnaw on his own bitterness. The household is a gallery of strangeness: a young woman of cold beauty who refuses to speak, a rough manservant named Joseph who dispenses scripture like curses, and an air of old, unresolved passion clinging to the stone walls.

“A perfect misanthropist’s heaven” — Lockwood's first impression of the Heights.— Ch. 1, Lockwood narrating

Trapped by a snowstorm, Lockwood is forced to spend the night. He sleeps in a closet-bed and discovers old diaries scratched into the windowsill paint — the name Catherine, over and over. In a dream half-waking, a small hand claws at the frozen glass. He wrenches the wrist across the broken pane and a child's voice begs to be let in after twenty years of wandering. He wakes, shaking. Heathcliff, summoned by the noise, rushes to the window and cries into the dark with an anguish that no visitor's curiosity can begin to explain.

Narrative frame Brontë tells the story twice-removed: Lockwood records what the housekeeper Nelly Dean tells him. This distance lets the reader judge both narrators — neither is wholly reliable, and the truth of Heathcliff and Catherine's love must be assembled from their partial testimony.

Chapter II · The Foundling

Nelly Dean has lived at the Heights since childhood. She tells Lockwood how, some thirty years earlier, old Mr Earnshaw returned from a journey to Liverpool with a ragged dark-skinned boy he had found starving in the streets. He named the boy Heathcliff — a name that would serve as both given name and surname, the name of a dead son — and declared he would raise him as his own.

The household divided at once. Hindley, Earnshaw's son, burned with jealousy at the interloper who displaced him in his father's affections. But Catherine, the wild, headstrong daughter, was immediately drawn to the boy. They were inseparable from their first days together, roaming the moors in defiance of weather and propriety alike, forming a bond that felt older than circumstance.

“He seemed a sullen, patient child; hardened, perhaps, to ill-treatment.”— Ch. 4, Nelly Dean

When Earnshaw died, Hindley inherited the Heights and immediately began his revenge: Heathcliff was stripped of his education, turned into a servant, forbidden the company of the gentry. But degradation only hardened the bond between Heathcliff and Catherine. The moors became their kingdom — beyond class, beyond family, beyond any law the house could impose.

Origins and belonging Heathcliff's race, parentage, and origin are never explained — deliberately. Brontë makes him legible as Irish famine refugee, Romani traveller, or freed slave. His irreducible otherness is the engine of the novel: he is the person the English class system refuses to accommodate, and he will make that system pay.

Chapter III · Love on the Moor

Peering through the windows of Thrushcross Grange one evening, Catherine and Heathcliff spy on the Linton children — Edgar and Isabella — squabbling over a lap-dog in a room blazing with candlelight and crimson carpet. The two worlds could not be more different: the Lintons represent everything polished and possessed; Catherine and Heathcliff, wild and dispossessed, watch from the cold dark.

Catherine is caught by the Lintons' dog and injured. She is taken inside and kept for five weeks to recover; Heathcliff is turned away as a guttersnipe. When Catherine returns, she has absorbed some of the Grange's culture — its refinements, its manners — and something between them has imperceptibly shifted. She is still Catherine, but she is also becoming someone else. Heathcliff watches, and begins to understand what class costs.

“I am Heathcliff — he’s always, always in my mind.”— Ch. 9, Catherine to Nelly Dean

Catherine confides to Nelly Dean that she has accepted Edgar Linton's proposal of marriage. She insists her love for Edgar is like the leaves — changeable, earthly. But her love for Heathcliff is like the eternal rocks beneath — a source of little visible delight, but necessary. Heathcliff, hiding in the kitchen and hearing only the first half of the speech, steals out into the night before she can finish. He does not hear her say he is more herself than she is.

The divided self Catherine's famous declaration — "I am Heathcliff" — is not romantic hyperbole. It describes a metaphysical claim: that two selves have become one, that separation is not heartbreak but self-annihilation. Brontë takes this literally. Catherine does not mourn Heathcliff's departure; she goes mad from the fracture of her own being.

Chapter IV · Humiliation & Flight

The wedding of Catherine and Edgar Linton goes ahead. Heathcliff, humiliated at every turn by Hindley — who has by now drunk himself into ruin since the death of his wife Frances — disappears without warning into the night. Three years pass. Catherine settles into a kind of contentment at the Grange, becoming mistress of the house, suppressing the wildness that was always her truest self. It is a performance, and Nelly Dean can see the cost.

Meanwhile, Hindley sinks further into dissolution at the Heights. He gambles, drinks, neglects his son Hareton, and treats the estate with contempt. The Heights decays with him. The only person who seems to benefit from Hindley's destruction is Heathcliff — who is, of course, not yet there to collect.

“He had been used to sleeping in a room with Hindley as a child... he was now made to sleep with the horses.”— Ch. 6, Nelly, recounting the degradation
The wound of class Hindley's persecution of Heathcliff is the novel's original violence — the point at which the class system acts through a petty individual to crush something extraordinary. Everything that follows is consequence. Brontë does not excuse Heathcliff; she insists we understand the arithmetic of cause and effect.

Chapter V · The Return

Three years after his flight, Heathcliff returns — and returns transformed. He is tall, well-made, well-dressed; he carries himself with a cold authority that commands respect where before he invited contempt. He says nothing of where he has been, how he has prospered, or what he intends. He says only that he has come back.

Catherine is transported with joy. Edgar is discomfited — he recognises the man, cannot account for the transformation, and is immediately, correctly, afraid. Heathcliff takes lodgings at the Heights, where Hindley welcomes the deep-pocketed newcomer to his card table without recognising, or caring about, the danger. Heathcliff begins, methodically, to win the Heights from him.

“He’s a dark-skinned gypsy in aspect, in dress and manners a gentleman.”— Ch. 7, Nelly Dean on the returned Heathcliff

Catherine insists on seeing Heathcliff. Edgar objects. She falls dangerously ill — refusing food, throwing herself into a fever that is as much psychological as physical. Brontë presents the illness not as melodrama but as fact: Catherine Earnshaw cannot live in contradiction of her own nature, and her nature is Heathcliff. Edgar and Heathcliff face each other across her suffering like opposing poles of her divided self.

Gothic transformation Heathcliff's mysterious enrichment is the novel's deliberate blank — Brontë never explains it. Readers have imagined everything from soldiering to slave-trading. The omission is the point: Heathcliff is a force the narrative refuses to fully domesticate. His return is not reconciliation; it is the beginning of a systematic settling of accounts.

Chapter VI · Revenge Unfolds

Heathcliff elopes with Isabella Linton — Edgar's naively romantic sister — not from desire but from calculation. She is his means of access to the Linton fortune and a wound in Edgar's flank. He makes no secret of his contempt for her. When Isabella writes to Nelly, horrified and disillusioned, describing the violence and degradation of her marriage, it reads like a dispatch from a prisoner who did not understand she was entering a cell.

Catherine, meanwhile, collapses entirely. The illness that began as will becomes organic; the doctors speak of brain fever. She is pregnant. In her delirium she imagines herself back at the Heights as a child, she and Heathcliff together on the moor, before any of it — before class, before Edgar, before the division that is killing her. She dies giving birth to a daughter, young Cathy.

“May she wake in torment!” — Heathcliff's curse upon Catherine for choosing Edgar.— Ch. 16

Heathcliff, admitted to the dying Catherine at last, accuses her of breaking both their hearts by marrying Edgar. He holds her. She tells him he has killed her and she does not care. He tells her not to leave him. She leaves. He begs her ghost to haunt him forever — a beggar's prayer, and one the novel takes seriously. Standing over her grave, alone in the dark, he tears at the earth and speaks to the air.

Love as destruction Heathcliff does not love Catherine gently. His love is total, possessive, and finally lethal — to her and, by the novel's end, to himself. Brontë does not romanticise this. She presents it as a fact of nature: certain forces are incompatible with survival in a social world, and will burn themselves out. The question is what they consume on the way.

Chapter VII · The Second Generation

The years pass. Young Cathy — Catherine and Edgar's daughter — grows up at Thrushcross Grange, pretty, clever, and sheltered. Her father's great terror is that she will wander toward the Heights. She does, of course, discover the moors; she discovers Hareton Earnshaw, Hindley's son, degraded by Heathcliff's deliberate neglect into illiteracy and roughness; and she discovers Linton Heathcliff, Isabella's son, who has returned to his father after his mother's death — a pale, peevish, sickly boy who is both pawn and victim.

Heathcliff, seeing history before him, manufactures a romance between young Cathy and Linton — his frail, compliant son. The goal is direct: if Cathy marries Linton, then upon Linton's near-inevitable early death, Thrushcross Grange passes to Heathcliff. He will own both properties. He will possess everything the Earnshaws and Lintons ever had.

“I want him to be my heir, and I want him to acquire wealth and position for me.”— Ch. 20, Heathcliff on Linton

Edgar Linton, dying, tries to prevent the marriage by appealing to the courts to alter his will. He is too late. Heathcliff lures Cathy to the Heights on a false pretext, keeps her captive long enough to force the wedding, and Edgar dies without seeing his daughter again. The trap closes. The revenge, taking twenty-five years to build, is now complete — on paper.

The inheritance of suffering The second generation re-enacts the first — but as tragedy becomes mechanism. Hareton is Heathcliff: the capable, passionate person denied education and dignity by a vindictive guardian. Cathy is Catherine: the bright woman constrained by circumstance. Linton Heathcliff is the cruelest irony — a son Heathcliff cannot love, a weapon he is compelled to use.

Chapter VIII · Ruin & Release

Linton Heathcliff dies, as expected, months after the forced marriage. Young Cathy is now a widow and a prisoner at the Heights, subject to the man who destroyed her father. But something unexpected is happening. Heathcliff, who has won everything, finds he cannot collect.

Looking at Hareton, he sees Catherine — her eyes in the young man's face. Looking at young Cathy, he sees her too. The objects of his revenge are mirrors of the people he loved and destroyed. He loses his appetite. He cannot eat. He begins to see Catherine everywhere — waking, in the dark, on the moor. She is in every face he turns toward.

“I have to remind myself to breathe — almost to remind my heart to beat.”— Ch. 33, Heathcliff to Nelly Dean

Meanwhile, young Cathy begins teaching Hareton to read. The tenderness between them — unlikely, tentative — is the novel's only new growth. It is not a repetition of their parents; it is something the second generation has built against the grain of their inheritance. Heathcliff, watching them, experiences something he cannot name.

He stops eating entirely. He dies alone in Catherine's old room, the window open, rain-wet, his face — Nelly says — wearing an expression not of suffering but of fierce exultation. He is buried, as he asked, beside Catherine, with the sides of both coffins removed so that they mingle in the earth. The country people say they walk the moors together still.

Dissolution as reunion Heathcliff does not die of grief in any clinical sense — he dies of completion. He has fulfilled the only desire that ever animated him: to be with Catherine, beyond the body, beyond class, beyond the social world that kept them apart. Whether the ghost-sightings are real or the projection of guilty minds, Brontë leaves carefully, lovingly open.

✦ Why it Matters

The central argument

  • Brontë insists that the deepest love is not compatible with the social world — it can only be fulfilled in death or dissolution.
  • Class is not background texture but the machine that produces all the novel's violence; Heathcliff's revenge is the mirror the system deserves.
  • The two narrative generations test whether cycles of damage can be broken — and tentatively suggest they can, but only through tenderness and education.
  • The novel refuses resolution: Heathcliff is simultaneously monster and victim, and Brontë never blinks at either.

Love & Self

Catherine's "I am Heathcliff" is the novel's philosophical centre. Brontë imagines love not as feeling but as identity — to love is to be constituted by another person, and loss is therefore not grief but fragmentation.

Class & Violence

Every act of Heathcliff's revenge traces back to a precise prior act of class humiliation. Brontë constructs the revenge with the rigour of a moral accountant — and then complicates it by showing what revenge costs its architect.

Nature vs Society

The moor is the novel's moral landscape — amoral, ungovernable, alive. Every character who belongs to it (Catherine, Heathcliff) is destroyed by the effort of living in society. Every character who belongs to society (Edgar, Isabella) is destroyed by contact with the moor.

The Uncanny

Whether Catherine haunts Heathcliff literally or psychologically, the novel treats the question seriously. Brontë does not close the supernatural door; she leaves it open on the dark, and that ambiguity is itself an argument about how the past inhabits us.

Why it endures

1847 Published under the pseudonym Ellis Bell
180+ Years in continuous print
1 novel Emily Brontë's only work of fiction
  • Critics initially condemned it as brutal and structurally confusing; within a generation it was canonised.
  • Kate Bush's 1978 song brought the novel to a global audience who had never read it — and sent them back to the text.
  • It sits at the intersection of Romanticism and Gothic fiction and belongs comfortably to neither.
  • It is one of the only canonical novels in which the romantic lead is plausibly read as a person of colour, making its racial politics permanently modern.
  • The double-narrative structure — Lockwood, Nelly, the diaries — anticipates modernist experiments in unreliable narration by fifty years.

Three most famous passages

“I am Heathcliff — he’s always, always in my mind.”

This is not a declaration of romantic feeling but an ontological claim. Catherine is not saying she loves Heathcliff; she is saying she is him — that her selfhood is constituted by his existence. The sentence rewrites what a love story can be.

“Be with me always — take any form — drive me mad.”

Heathcliff over Catherine's grave. This is the novel's emotional summit — the moment when the love-plot becomes openly supernatural. He is not asking for comfort; he is demanding haunting. It is the most unguarded sentence in the book.

“I have to remind myself to breathe — almost to remind my heart to beat.”

Heathcliff near death. The sentence describes not grief but the literal failure of the body when the animating purpose is exhausted. Brontë turns a metaphor — heartbreak — back into physiology. The revenge is done; the engine stops.
