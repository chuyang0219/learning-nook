Crime and Punishment
Fyodor Dostoevsky

I · Summer, St. Petersburg
The idea
A man hatches a plan he cannot stop thinking about

High summer in St. Petersburg, 1865. The city stinks. The heat is a physical weight. In a garret no larger than a cupboard — yellow walls, low ceiling, a sofa he has not left for days — Rodion Romanovich Raskolnikov lies thinking. He is twenty-three. He has pawned his watch, his coat. He has not eaten properly in weeks. And still the idea will not leave him.

It began as a thought experiment, as the most dangerous thoughts always do. It has become something he cannot look away from.

Raskolnikov's theory — what he has convinced himself of

1.The division: All people belong to one of two categories. The ordinary — the mass of humanity, governed and passive — must obey the law. The extraordinary — the Napoleons, the Newtons — are its makers. They have always transgressed when they needed to, and history has called it genius.
2.The justification: There is a pawnbroker — Alyona Ivanovna — on the Voznesensky Prospect. A louse. She leeches off the desperate, and her money will go to a monastery when she dies. Kill her. Take the money. Study. Become someone who saves thousands. One trivial crime against a useless life.
3.The real question: And yet — he knows this is a rationalisation, has always known it. What he is truly asking is something else: am I the kind of man who can do this? Am I extraordinary, or merely ordinary — like everyone else? The murder is a test of his own nature.
4.The flaw he does not see: The theory assumes that a sufficiently great man would feel nothing — that conscience is a convention of the herd, not a part of the self. The entire novel that follows is Dostoevsky's answer to that assumption.
"Power is given only to him who dares to stoop and take it... one must have the courage to dare."— Raskolnikov, Part I
II · The act
The murder
The extraordinary man puts his theory to the test

Raskolnikov has borrowed an axe. He has rehearsed the route, the door, the words he will use to get her to open it. His hands are cold; his head is very clear. Going up the stairs, he tells himself one last time:

"What do you think, would not one tiny crime be wiped out by thousands of good deeds?"— Raskolnikov's self-justification, Part I

She opens the door. He kills Alyona Ivanovna with the blunt edge of the axe. Then he stands in her apartment, looking for the keys to her strongbox.

The door opens. Her half-sister Lizaveta has come home early, arms full of parcels. She stares at him. She raises one hand, feebly, as if to ward off a blow. She had not been part of the calculation at all. He kills her too.

By extraordinary luck — a tenant rings the bell just as he finishes — he slips down the stairs past two men on their way up, returns the axe, reaches his garret, and loses consciousness.

The fatal complication

Lizaveta was everything the theory was supposed to protect: poor, exploited, harmless. Her death quietly demolishes his entire philosophical justification — and he has not even left the apartment when it happens.

the axe — instrument & symbol
blood — guilt made visible
the door — point of no return
III · The aftermath
The fever
The murderer is undone not by police — but by his own mind

He does not escape unpunished. He escapes only into himself — which is worse.

For days he lies in fever, muttering, delirious. His friend Razumikhin — steady, warm, decent in the way Raskolnikov is not — nurses him without knowing what has happened. When Raskolnikov finally surfaces, pale and shaking, the theory that was supposed to leave him free has instead taken everything from him.

"It takes something more than intelligence to act intelligently."— the novel's quiet verdict on Raskolnikov's entire project

A summons arrives from the police station. He goes in terror — only to find it concerns an unpaid debt. But while he is there, he overhears two clerks talking about the murdered pawnbroker. The room tilts. He faints.

This is the shape his guilt will take for the rest of the novel: not confession, not flight, but a perpetual half-fainting, always on the edge of giving himself away.

"To go wrong in one's own way is better than to go right in someone else's."— Razumikhin, Part III
IV · The other world
Sonya
The saint of the gutter — the novel's true moral centre

Before the murder — in a tavern, of all places — Raskolnikov had stumbled into a drunk's company. Marmeladov: a ruined civil servant, magnificently wretched, who wanted to confess his particular degradation to a stranger.

"Do you understand, sir, do you understand what it means when you have absolutely nowhere to turn? For every man must have somewhere to turn."— Marmeladov to Raskolnikov, Part I

Marmeladov had a daughter, Sonya. Poverty had driven her to sell herself so that her stepmother's children might eat. Some weeks after this meeting, Marmeladov is struck by a horse-carriage and dies. Raskolnikov — impulsively — pays for his funeral. This act brings him to Sonya's door.

She is meek. She wears a cypress cross at her throat. She has been degraded by the world in every material way and remains, inexplicably, unbroken by it. When Raskolnikov eventually confesses to her, she does not pull away. She weeps.

The contrast Dostoevsky is drawing

Raskolnikov crossed a moral boundary by choice, to prove his freedom. Sonya crossed an equally terrible one through necessity, with no theory to justify it — and remained spiritually whole. The same transgression. Opposite souls.

V · The doubles
Svidrigailov
The man who committed Raskolnikov's crime — and felt nothing

The narrator could be describing either man when he writes:

"Existence alone had never been enough for him; he had always wanted more. Perhaps it was only from the force of his desires that he had regarded himself as a man to whom more was permitted than to others."— on Raskolnikov; equally true of Svidrigailov

Svidrigailov arrives in Petersburg trailing rumours. He is the former employer of Raskolnikov's sister Dunya, from whose household she had fled under unexplained circumstances. His wife died — also under circumstances. He is charming, wealthy, and completely amoral — Raskolnikov's dark mirror. He has crossed every line Raskolnikov only theorised about, and emerged utterly hollow. He performs occasional acts of generosity not from goodness but from boredom.

When he finally meets Raskolnikov and recognises him immediately, he is delighted. At last: company.

"We are birds of a feather," Svidrigailov tells Raskolnikov with a smile.— Part IV, Chapter I
What Svidrigailov proves

Everything Raskolnikov's theory promised — freedom from scruple, from conventional guilt — Svidrigailov already has. He is the proof of concept. And he is trivial, jaded, and spiritually dead. The extraordinary man turns out to be merely the empty one.

VI · The cat and mouse
Porfiry
A detective who doesn't need evidence — he needs confession

The detective assigned to the pawnbroker's murder is Porfiry Petrovich. He is round, mild-mannered, inclined to laugh at his own jokes, and he knows — before he has a single piece of evidence — that Raskolnikov is his man. He knows because Raskolnikov had published a student article, months before the murder, laying out the precise philosophical justification for exactly this kind of crime. Porfiry found it interesting.

Across three separate interview scenes, he plays with Raskolnikov the way a cat plays with something it has already caught. He does not accuse. He praises. He asks innocent questions. He laughs. He leaves long, terrible pauses. He apologises for taking up Raskolnikov's time. Then he adds one more observation.

"A hundred suspicions don't make a proof."— Razumikhin, Part IV

Raskolnikov can see every move before it is made. He cannot stop making them. In their final confrontation, Porfiry dispenses with the pretence entirely.

"The psychology is a two-edged weapon... You are the murderer, Rodion Romanovich. You yourself are the murderer."— Porfiry Petrovich, Part VI
Dostoevsky's masterstroke

Porfiry does not want merely to arrest Raskolnikov. He wants him to confess of his own free will — not to escape punishment but because voluntary suffering is, in his view, the only thing that can save Raskolnikov's soul. "Go and confess," he tells him. "It will be better. For yourself." He is simultaneously a detective and a reluctant priest.

"Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart. The really great men must, I think, have great sadness on earth."— Porfiry to Raskolnikov, Part IV
VII · The turning point
Lazarus
Raskolnikov asks Sonya to read him scripture

Raskolnikov has confessed to Sonya. Now — he doesn't know why, exactly — he asks her to read to him. She takes up the New Testament and finds the passage he has asked for: the raising of Lazarus. A man four days dead. A tomb sealed with a stone. And then a voice, and a man walking out blinking into the light.

Sonya reads it as she believes it — literally, entirely, without embarrassment. Raskolnikov listens. He does not believe it. He asks her to read it again. Then, before he has decided to, he is on his knees before her.

"I did not bow down to you, I bowed down to all the suffering of humanity."— Raskolnikov, Part IV
The novel's spiritual architecture

Dostoevsky — sentenced to death himself, exiled to Siberia for four years — structures the novel as a passion: sin, suffering, and the possibility of resurrection. But resurrection is not given. It must be chosen, and chosen again, at great cost.

VIII · The reckoning
Two endings
One man shoots himself. The other walks to the police station.

Svidrigailov makes his final attempt on Dunya — corners her, refuses to let her leave, and discovers, when she produces a revolver and fires, that she will not be kept. The bullet grazes the air beside his head. He looks at her for a long moment. Then he tells her she may go.

That night he moves through the city giving things away — money to Sonya, money to orphans, money to his young fiancée. At dawn he finds a night watchman near an ornamental watchtower and engages him in brief conversation.

"I'm going to America," he said. Then he put the pistol to his right temple.— Part VI

That same morning, Raskolnikov goes to Sonya. Before he leaves, she tells him what he must do:

"Go to the crossroads, bow down to the people, kiss the earth, for you have sinned against it too, and say aloud to the whole world: I am a murderer."— Sonya to Raskolnikov, Part V

He goes to the Haymarket, kneels in the dirt, and kisses the earth while people stare. Then he walks to the police station.

"It was I who killed the old pawnbroker woman and her sister Lizaveta with an axe and robbed them."— Raskolnikov's confession, Part VI
IX · Siberia
Resurrection
Or its first uncertain glimmer

Eight years' hard labour in Siberia. Sonya follows him voluntarily — obtains permission, settles in the nearby town, visits when she can. The other convicts, who hate Raskolnikov for his coldness and his educated pride, are inexplicably tender with her.

For the first year, Raskolnikov does not repent. He submits to punishment but not to guilt. He had been right, in theory; he had simply been too weak in practice — proved himself ordinary after all. This is cold comfort, but he holds it.

Then it is spring. The river is running. Raskolnikov is standing on the bank when Sonya approaches, and something breaks in him — not dramatically, not with revelation, but the way a long illness sometimes simply ends.

"How it happened he did not know. But all at once something seemed to seize him and fling him at her feet. He wept and threw his arms around her knees... He loved her, and his heart was renewed."— Epilogue

The novel does not continue past this moment. A new story could begin here — the story of Raskolnikov's gradual return to life — but this novel belongs to crime and its aftermath, and so it ends.

What Sonya said to him, earlier, when he confessed

"Your worst sin is that you have destroyed and betrayed yourself for nothing." This is not condemnation. It is the most precise description of what the theory cost him — not the victims' lives, terrible as that is, but his own.

Essay · Why it endures
What the novel ultimately argues
Ideas, legacy, and the most famous passages
The central argument
Suffering is not punishment — it is the only path back to being human. Conscience cannot be reasoned away; it is the self.
The theory that freed Raskolnikov from guilt destroyed him. Accepting guilt begins to rebuild him.
Sonya's love is not sentimental — it is the only force in the book stronger than pride.
Freedom through transgression leads to Svidrigailov's emptiness. Freedom through submission leads to Raskolnikov's first breath of life.
Key ideas
Ideas have consequences
Raskolnikov acts not from passion or poverty but from a philosophical idea. Abstract ideas divorced from human feeling become monstrous when lived out.
The extraordinary man
The theory anticipates Nietzsche's Übermensch — written a decade later. Dostoevsky's answer: conscience is not a social convention to be reasoned away. It is the self.
Freedom through submission
The great paradox: the only freedom Raskolnikov finds is through confession and acceptance. Attempting to transcend limits produced imprisonment. Accepting limits produces release.
Psychology of guilt
The first novel to make the interior experience of guilt — not its consequences — its central subject. Every psychological thriller since inherits this.

Three most famous passages

"Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart. The really great men must, I think, have great sadness on earth."

— Porfiry Petrovich

"To go wrong in one's own way is better than to go right in someone else's."

— Razumikhin · A throwaway remark in an argument about independent thought. It crystallises one of the novel's deepest themes: authentic error is more human — and more redeemable — than borrowed correctness. It applies to Raskolnikov's theory as much as to anything else.

"It takes something more than intelligence to act intelligently."

· The quiet epigraph for Raskolnikov's catastrophe. He constructed his justification with perfect logical rigour. What he lacked was not cleverness but humanity — the felt weight of another person's life.

"The darker the night, the brighter the stars, the deeper the grief, the closer is God."

— Dostoevsky, A Raw Youth (1875) · frequently but wrongly attributed to Crime and Punishment
