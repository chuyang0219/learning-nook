I · The Bennets of Longbourn

The opening truth · Five daughters · The marriage market

The novel announces itself with one of literature's most famous ironies: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife." Austen's tone is perfectly judged — she says "truth universally acknowledged" about a "truth" that is entirely a social construction, spoken by exactly the people who most need to believe it.

Mrs Bennet is one of those people. She has five daughters and a husband's estate entailed away from them: when Mr Bennet dies, the family loses its home. Marriage for the Bennet girls is not a romance — it is an economic necessity. Austen understands this and never lets the reader forget it, even while the novel is busy being delightful.

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

— Opening line, Chapter 1

A new tenant has arrived at Netherfield Park: Mr Bingley, young, amiable, and possessed of five thousand a year. Mrs Bennet is beside herself. Mr Bennet has already called on him and withheld this information from his wife for the pure pleasure of watching her arrive at it. This is the marriage of Mr and Mrs Bennet, compressed into a single scene.

The Social Architecture

Austen builds her comedy on a foundation of genuine economic precarity. The Bennets are genteel but unrich, and the entail — a legal mechanism that prevented women from inheriting property — was a real and common source of female vulnerability. The novel is funny because Austen's irony is perfect; it is also serious because the underlying situation is not funny at all.

II · First Impressions

The Netherfield ball · Darcy's slight · Prejudice formed

At the Meryton assembly, Mr Darcy arrives with Bingley. He is immediately recognised as more wealthy, more handsome, and more insufferable than his friend. He dances only with those in his own party, refuses introductions, and is overheard declining to dance with Elizabeth Bennet on the grounds that she is "tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me."

Elizabeth hears this. She retells it as a joke — and this is her double-edged gift. Her intelligence transforms slights into comedy, which protects her and makes her excellent company but also makes it easier to dismiss what she should examine. She writes Darcy off as proud. He is proud. But he is also, she does not yet see, already watching her.

"She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me."

— Mr Darcy, Chapter 3

Jane Bennet and Mr Bingley, meanwhile, are immediately, mutually delighted with each other. Jane sees only the best in everyone. Elizabeth sees too clearly. The novel is, in part, about the distance between those two ways of looking.

First Impressions — the Novel's Original Title

Austen's working title was "First Impressions." The final title is more precise about the book's double subject: it takes both characters to form the couple, which means both must change — Darcy must surrender his pride, Elizabeth her prejudice. Neither is wholly wrong; each is wrong enough to make them miserable.

III · Collins and Wickham

The proposal refused · The charmer believed

Mr Collins arrives at Longbourn. He is Mr Bennet's heir, and he has arrived to choose a wife from among the daughters who will lose their home to him. His choice falls on Elizabeth. His proposal is a masterpiece of comic self-importance: he enumerates his own virtues at length, assures Elizabeth he will forget her family's social deficiencies, and is genuinely baffled when she says no.

He transfers his attentions to Charlotte Lucas, Elizabeth's best friend, who accepts him within three days. Elizabeth is appalled. Charlotte is pragmatic: she is twenty-seven, plain, and without prospects. A comfortable home and an irritating husband is a rational outcome. Austen does not mock Charlotte for this. She understands the arithmetic.

"I am not romantic, you know; I never was. I ask only a comfortable home."

— Charlotte Lucas, Chapter 22

Meanwhile, Mr Wickham joins the militia and attaches himself to Elizabeth. He is charming, sympathetic, and fluent in grievance — he tells her that Darcy deprived him of a living promised by Darcy's father. Elizabeth believes him. She wants to. It confirms her reading of Darcy as proud and villainous, which means she can stop examining it.

Theme: Performance and Sincerity

Austen's world is one of social performance. Collins performs deference. Wickham performs injury. Darcy performs disdain. The novel's task is to teach Elizabeth — and the reader — to distinguish performance from character, which requires attending to what people do, not how they present themselves.

IV · Hunsford and Rosings

Lady Catherine · The first proposal · The letter

Elizabeth visits Charlotte at Hunsford Parsonage. There she encounters Lady Catherine de Bourgh — Darcy's aunt, Collins's patron, and a woman of such magnificent self-certainty that she constitutes a kind of satire on the whole aristocratic idea. She interrogates her guests about their accomplishments and finds them wanting.

Darcy is visiting too. He calls at the parsonage more often than is explicable. Then, abruptly, he proposes — badly. He cannot hide his sense that he is condescending, that the match is beneath him, that he has struggled against it and lost. He expects gratitude. Elizabeth says no, with feeling.

"You could not have made the offer of your hand in any possible way that would have tempted me to accept it."

— Elizabeth Bennet, Chapter 34

Darcy writes her a letter. It is the pivot of the novel. He explains Wickham — the truth of the man, his lies and his attempted elopement with Georgiana Darcy. And he explains his intervention to separate Bingley from Jane, taking full responsibility. Elizabeth reads it twice. She begins to see that she has been wrong — about Wickham, about Darcy, and about the quality of her own judgment.

The Letter: The Novel's Turning Point

Austen is precise about what the letter does: it forces Elizabeth to re-examine not just Darcy but herself. "How despicably have I acted!" she reflects — and the novel's great irony begins to turn. Her prejudice has been as blinding as his pride. Both must be dismantled before anything real can begin.

V · Pemberley

The estate · The true Darcy · Lydia's elopement

Travelling through Derbyshire with her aunt and uncle, the Gardiners, Elizabeth visits Pemberley. She has been told Darcy is away. The house is everything his character, seen properly, would predict: grand but not showy, perfectly proportioned, situated beautifully. The housekeeper speaks of him with genuine warmth, not sycophancy. Elizabeth begins to feel that she has misjudged quite thoroughly.

Darcy arrives. He is courteous to her relatives, attentive without pressure, and nothing like the man she refused. The re-evaluation is almost complete when — in rapid succession — a letter arrives: Lydia has eloped with Wickham. The family is ruined unless the marriage can be secured. Elizabeth tells Darcy. He leaves immediately.

"She began now to comprehend that he was exactly the man who, in disposition and talents, would most suit her."

— Narrator, Chapter 43

She does not yet know that Darcy has gone to find Wickham, pay his debts, and make the marriage possible — rescuing the Bennets entirely, quietly, without telling anyone. Elizabeth learns this through her aunt. Austen notes simply that Elizabeth had never been so sensible of the difference between action and performance.

Pemberley as Character

The house works as revelation. Austen argues that how a person inhabits and uses their wealth is moral information. Pemberley is "handsome" but not "overdone" — it shows taste without vanity. Elizabeth's response to it is the moment her feelings begin to change. The novel suggests this is correct: character is readable in its objects.

VI · The Resolution

Lady Catherine repelled · The second proposal · Four weddings

Lady Catherine de Bourgh arrives at Longbourn in high indignation. She has heard rumours of an engagement between Darcy and Elizabeth and has come to demand it be denied. Elizabeth refuses to deny it. She will not be intimidated by rank and will not be told by another person whom she is permitted to love.

Lady Catherine goes away and tells Darcy of Elizabeth's refusal to comply. Darcy, who had given up any hope of being accepted, understands from this that Elizabeth has not closed the door. He walks to Longbourn and tries again. This time he does not enumerate his condescension. He says he loves her and asks if her feelings have changed.

"My affections and wishes are unchanged, but one word from you will silence me on this subject for ever."

— Mr Darcy, Chapter 58

Her feelings have changed. The novel ends with four marriages: Elizabeth and Darcy, Jane and Bingley, Lydia and Wickham (secured by Darcy's money), and — somewhere offstage — the permanent comedy of Mr and Mrs Bennet. Austen distributes these outcomes with precision: the marriages that will produce happiness are those based on genuine knowledge; the rest are what happens when people marry for the wrong reasons, or no reasons at all.

The Resolution's Terms

Darcy's second proposal is short, honest, and entirely without self-pity. It is the proof that he has changed. Elizabeth's acceptance is not capitulation — she has been the novel's moral authority throughout. What changes is her knowledge. They are perfectly matched: two people of intelligence and strong will who have finally seen each other clearly.

Why It Matters

Legacy · Themes · Famous passages

Pride and Prejudice is the funniest serious novel in English — and the seriousness is what makes the humour last. Austen invented romantic comedy as a form by insisting it operate as moral philosophy: her people do not simply fall in love; they earn it by becoming more accurate about themselves and each other.

  • The novel argues that economic reality and romantic happiness are not necessarily opposed — but getting both requires intelligence and self-knowledge
  • Austen's irony creates a double readership: the characters believe one thing while the reader sees another, and the gap between them is the comedy
  • Elizabeth Bennet's refusal to be diminished by lack of wealth or social position is the novel's feminist argument, made through action rather than polemic
  • The novel diagnoses two complementary failures of perception — pride closes the eyes; prejudice colours them — and requires both to be corrected before happiness is possible

Irony as Method

Austen's signature technique: the narrative voice says one thing, means another, and trusts the reader to notice. This requires an alert reader — and rewards them.

The Marriage Question

Every marriage in the novel is a test case. Austen examines what motives produce what outcomes: mercenary, impulsive, affectionate, intelligent — all represented.

Class and Character

Rank is not merit. Wickham is charming; Darcy is stiff. The novel insists on distinguishing between how people perform socially and what they actually are.

Female Intelligence

Elizabeth's wit and perception are never once framed as unfeminine. Austen's heroines are allowed to be clever; it is their intelligence the reader is invited to admire.

Three Famous Passages

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

The most famous opening sentence in English fiction — not because it states a truth but because it anatomises a social delusion. The irony is in "universally acknowledged," which identifies who exactly does the acknowledging.

"In vain I have struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

Darcy's first proposal — magnificent in its failure. He tells Elizabeth he loves her while simultaneously explaining that he wishes he did not. It is entirely honest and entirely disastrous, and Austen makes it both at once.

"Till this moment I never knew myself."

Elizabeth, reading Darcy's letter. The shortest and most devastating sentence in the novel. Six words that do the work of a conversion scene — she sees herself, possibly for the first time, as others see her.
