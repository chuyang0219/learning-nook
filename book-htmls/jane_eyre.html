I · Gateshead

Childhood · The red room · The first defiance

She is ten years old, an orphan in a house that regards her as a burden. Jane Eyre lives with her aunt, Mrs Reed, and three cousins who have every advantage she lacks — money, status, and the presumed right to cruelty. John Reed strikes her and tells her she belongs in the poorhouse. Jane strikes back.

The punishment is the red room: a locked chamber where her uncle died, cold and heavy with his memory. Alone in it, Jane experiences something close to breakdown — not from fear of ghosts, but from the injustice of her situation crystallised into a single terrible room. She is passionate, plain, and stubbornly herself. The Reeds cannot forgive her for any of it.

"I am no bird; and no net ensnares me."

— Jane Eyre, Vol. II

Mr Brocklehurst, the hypocritical clergyman who runs Lowood Institution, arrives at Gateshead. Mrs Reed tells him Jane is a liar — a charge that will follow her and which she spends the whole novel refuting with her irreproachable honesty. Jane is dispatched to school.

Theme: The Politics of the Powerless

From the opening pages, Brontë frames social inequality through a child's experience of it. Jane is not simply unlucky — she is subject to a system that routinely punishes the poor and the plain for existing. Her rage is not a character flaw; it is a correct reading of her situation.

II · Lowood School

Hunger · Friendship · The first death

Lowood is a charity school for orphaned girls — underfed, overcrowded, and governed by Mr Brocklehurst's brand of ostentatious piety that starves the body while preaching the soul. The food is burnt porridge and rancid meat. The cold is constant. Jane endures.

What saves her is Helen Burns: a girl of extraordinary equanimity who responds to injustice not with fury, like Jane, but with patient philosophical detachment. They become friends. Helen teaches Jane to endure what cannot be changed; Jane teaches the reader that some things should not be endured at all. The novel holds both positions simultaneously, without resolving them.

"Life appears to me too short to be spent in nursing animosity or registering wrongs."

— Helen Burns, Chapter 6

A typhus epidemic sweeps through Lowood's underfed population. Helen, already ill with tuberculosis, dies in Jane's arms one winter night. It is the first great loss of Jane's life, and it teaches her that the world will take from her without apology or explanation. Jane spends eight years at Lowood — six as pupil, two as teacher — before she grows restless and advertises for a governess position.

Theme: Moral Education vs. Social Obedience

Brontë distinguishes between two modes of instruction: Brocklehurst's, which uses religion to enforce submission, and Helen's, which uses philosophy to cultivate inner dignity. Jane finds neither wholly adequate — she wants justice, not mere endurance — but Helen shapes her moral vocabulary for the rest of the novel.

III · Thornfield Hall

The governess arrives · Rochester · The mystery above

Jane arrives at Thornfield Hall as governess to Adèle, the ward of its absent owner, Edward Rochester. The house is magnificent and strange — all dark wood and shuttered passages and a servant called Grace Poole whose presence no one adequately explains. Occasionally, from the upper floors, comes a laugh that sounds nothing like amusement.

Rochester arrives dramatically, thrown from his horse on the icy lane, and Jane — practical, unfrightened — helps him before she knows who he is. He is neither handsome nor polished; he speaks to her as an equal because she answers him as one. Something unprecedented begins. He seems to find in her a candour the drawing rooms of England cannot provide.

"Do you think, because I am poor, obscure, plain, and little, I am soulless and heartless?"

— Jane Eyre, Chapter 23

Society arrives at Thornfield in the form of Blanche Ingram and her mother, who treat Jane with the contempt reserved for those without money or position. Rochester uses Blanche to make Jane jealous, and the cruelty of it — even in the service of love — is noticed and filed away. Jane loves him. She is beginning to understand the danger of it.

Theme: Equality of Souls

The Thornfield chapters argue that true connection requires the acknowledgement of equal humanity, regardless of class and gender. Rochester is drawn to Jane precisely because she refuses to be impressed by his wealth or cowed by his authority. The relationship is revolutionary and, as the novel will show, not without its own forms of power play.

IV · The Secret

The proposal · The wedding stopped · Bertha

Rochester proposes to Jane in the garden at Thornfield. She accepts, incredulous, half-expecting to be mocked. He is not mocking her. But the weeks before the wedding are uneasy — Jane finds herself given expensive dresses and jewellery she doesn't want, transformed into something she is not. She gives the presents away. She understands, dimly, that love can be another form of enclosure.

At the altar, a stranger rises. Richard Mason declares that Rochester has a lawful wife living: Bertha Mason, his first wife, confined in the attic of Thornfield for a decade. The ceremony stops. Rochester does not deny it. He takes the wedding party to the attic and shows them Bertha — a woman of terrifying strength and absolute misery, reduced by years of imprisonment to something the novel's era called madness.

"I am no better than the old lightning-struck chestnut-tree in Thornfield orchard."

— Rochester, Chapter 27

Rochester pleads with Jane to stay — he did not love Bertha, was trapped into the marriage, and loves Jane truly. He is not wrong about loving her. He is wrong about what love grants him. Jane understands this more clearly than he does: to stay would be to become his mistress, to be kept, to lose the self she has built so painfully. She leaves in the grey of the morning, on foot, with no money and no plan.

The Novel's Central Crisis: Conscience vs. Passion

Bertha Mason has fascinated critics for two centuries. She is Rochester's first wife, imprisoned rather than acknowledged, reduced to a symbol of his past while Jane is offered as his future. Jean Rhys would later give Bertha her own voice in Wide Sargasso Sea (1966), arguing that the novel's moral architecture rests on the silencing of a West Indian woman. Brontë's Jane does the right thing — and the system that made the right thing necessary is what the novel judges.

V · Flight & the Moor

Destitution · Moor House · The inheritance

Jane walks away from Thornfield with thirty shillings and her principles. She boards a coach to somewhere unspecified, falls asleep, and is left by the roadside with nothing. The moors nearly kill her: she begs, is turned away, collapses in rain and darkness on the doorstep of a house called Moor House, Marsh End.

The inhabitants are St John Rivers and his sisters, Diana and Mary. They nurse her back to health, give her work as a village schoolteacher, and gradually reveal themselves as the cousins she never knew she had. An uncle, John Eyre, has died and left his entire fortune — twenty thousand pounds — to Jane. She divides it into four equal parts without hesitation.

"I would always rather be happy than dignified."

— Jane Eyre, Chapter 21

The moor sections are among the most unusual in Victorian fiction. Where other heroines find refuge in society, Jane finds it in landscape — alone, cold, stripped of all social identity, she is most purely herself. The moor does not judge her. It nearly destroys her, too, which Brontë seems to consider an acceptable price for that freedom.

Theme: Self-Possession Through Destitution

Jane's choice to leave Rochester is the moral crisis of the novel. Her destitution is the consequence. Brontë refuses to soften either — Jane suffers genuinely for her principles, and the novel does not suggest she should have done otherwise. The inheritance restores her material freedom, but she earns moral freedom on the moor.

VI · Moor House

St John Rivers · The missionary proposal · The voice on the wind

St John Rivers is handsome, cold, and certain of his divine vocation. He is everything Rochester is not: controlled, passionless, ambitious in the service of God rather than self. He plans to go to India as a missionary and he wants Jane to come as his wife. Not because he loves her. Because she would be useful.

Jane is drawn to his force of character even as she recognises its danger. He does not appeal to her desires — he appeals to her sense of duty, her guilt about happiness, her fear that she is not enough on her own. It is, in its way, more threatening than Rochester's passion: it asks her to extinguish herself in the name of a higher cause.

"If I were to marry you, you would kill me. You are killing me now."

— Jane Eyre, Chapter 34

Then it happens: across the moors at night, in a moment Brontë leaves deliberately ambiguous, Jane hears Rochester's voice calling her name. It is either supernatural, psychological, or — the novel does not choose — something that partakes of both. She turns from St John the next morning. She goes back.

St John as Anti-Rochester

Where Rochester's flaw is passion without conscience, St John's is conscience without passion — or rather, passion entirely redirected toward power. His missionary zeal is its own form of domination: he would possess Jane's soul rather than her body. Brontë constructs the choice between them as a choice between two modes of self-erasure, and makes Jane refuse both.

VII · Return

Thornfield in ruins · Ferndean · "Reader, I married him."

Jane finds Thornfield a burned ruin. Bertha Mason set fire to it one night, walked onto the roof, and leaped. Rochester lost his sight and one hand trying to save the servants. He retreated to a smaller house, Ferndean, in the deep woods. He is diminished. He does not know Jane is coming.

When she appears before him, blind and alone, the balance of the relationship shifts permanently. Rochester the powerful, Rochester the withholder of secrets, is dependent. Jane is rich, free, whole. She tells him she has come back. She asks him to marry her — the proposition, for once, is hers. He says yes immediately and with something that approaches awe.

"Reader, I married him."

— Jane Eyre, Chapter 38

The famous opening of the final chapter — "Reader, I married him" — has struck generations as a kind of victory cry. Jane has survived everything the world could do to her: cruelty, poverty, passion, temptation, bereavement. She has ended up in the only place she wanted to be, and on terms that are her own. Ten years on, Rochester's sight is partially restored. He sees his son. Jane narrates the whole of it with the quiet satisfaction of someone who has earned every word.

The Resolution: Equality, at Last

Critics have debated the ending's conservatism — Jane still marries and domesticates. But Brontë's point is the terms: Jane returns not as a supplicant but as Rochester's equal in wealth, moral authority, and emotional power. The novel ends exactly where it had to — with Jane speaking her own story in her own voice, directly to the reader, claiming the last word.

Why It Matters

Legacy · Themes · Famous passages

Jane Eyre invented the inner life of the Victorian novel's heroine. Before Jane, the protagonists of English fiction were largely acted upon; Jane acts. She is the first sustained first-person female voice in English fiction to possess — and insist on — a self that is not defined by her relationship to men.

  • Jane is the prototype of the modern psychological novel's narrator: unreliable in sentiment, rigorously honest in self-examination
  • The novel's treatment of class — through charity schools, governesses, and the inheritance — exposes the cruelty of Victorian social arrangements
  • Bertha Mason inaugurated a century of feminist literary criticism: she is the suppressed double of the Victorian woman, the passion that respectability requires imprisoned
  • The gothic mode — mad wives, burning houses, mysterious laughter — is not decoration but form: the unconscious expressed as architecture

The Governess

Neither servant nor family, the governess occupies an impossible social position. Jane's dignity within that position is a sustained argument that personhood transcends class.

The Madwoman

Bertha is what happens to women whose passion and agency are inconvenient. Her imprisonment is the dark underside of the Victorian domestic ideal.

The Moors

The Yorkshire landscape functions as an extension of Jane's psyche — wild, inhospitable to convention, and ultimately the only place where she is fully herself.

The Plain Heroine

Jane's plainness is insisted upon. Beauty would give her social currency she doesn't have; without it, everything she achieves must be won by character alone.

Three Famous Passages

"I am no bird; and no net ensnares me: I am a free human being with an independent will."

The fullest statement of Jane's selfhood, spoken to Rochester as she resists his attempts to define her by his love for her. It is the novel's thesis sentence.

"Reader, I married him."

The most famous opening line of a final chapter in English literature. Its directness — its refusal of circumlocution — is the culmination of Jane's voice: she claims the last word on her own life.

"Do you think, because I am poor, obscure, plain, and little, I am soulless and heartless?"

Jane's great declaration to Rochester before he proposes. It insists that the categories society uses to dismiss her — poverty, plainness, insignificance — are irrelevant to the question of her human worth.
