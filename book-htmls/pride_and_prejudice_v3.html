Pride and Prejudice

Jane Austen · 1813

Chapters
I · The Bennets
Hertfordshire, autumn — a single man of large fortune

It is a truth universally acknowledged that a single man in possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife — and so the news of Netherfield Park's new tenant reaches Mrs Bennet like a summons. She needs no further reasoning. Bingley is young, handsome, and worth five thousand a year; that is cause enough for celebration, and her eldest daughter Jane is the obvious candidate.

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

— Opening line, Chapter 1

Mr Bennet, a man of dry wit and studied detachment, has long since taken refuge in irony from a marriage more vigorous in nerves than in sense. He obliges his wife by calling on Bingley but takes pleasure in letting her suffer the suspense first. The five Bennet daughters — Jane, Elizabeth, Mary, Kitty, and Lydia — exist in a household whose only real urgency is matrimony. None of them will inherit Longbourn; the entail passes to a male cousin.

At the first neighbourhood assembly, Bingley arrives with his fashionable party, including his still more fashionable friend Mr Darcy. Bingley dances every dance; Darcy dances none. Bingley singles out Jane at once. Darcy, surveying the room with calm displeasure, declines to be introduced to Elizabeth — she is, he tells Bingley within her hearing, "tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me." Elizabeth overhears the slight, laughs at it, and repeats it to her friends. The evening ends with Mrs Bennet triumphant about Jane and Elizabeth cheerfully unimpressed by Darcy.

Mrs Bennet's campaign begins in earnest — and already the gap between what she says and what she means is the engine of every scene. Bingley is charming and pliant; Darcy's first impression of Elizabeth is the novel's first great irony, since we will watch that contempt turn slowly into something far more costly for his pride.

II · The Ball
Netherfield — Bingley's admiration, Darcy's slow awakening

Jane's visits to Netherfield become longer and closer. When she falls ill during a rainy call, she is installed as a houseguest, and Elizabeth — refusing to wait decorously — walks three muddy miles to nurse her. The visit places Elizabeth squarely in Darcy's company for several days. He finds himself watching her, then catching himself watching her, then becoming irritated that he watches her at all.

Darcy's companions, Bingley's sisters, are delighted to condescend to Elizabeth in the drawing room and whisper against her behind her back. They find Darcy's apparent attention to her ridiculous. Darcy, who has been telling himself he merely admires the quickness of her wit as an intellectual curiosity, begins transcribing her eyes into letters he does not send.

"She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me."

— Mr Darcy, overheard by Elizabeth, Chapter 3

The Netherfield ball arrives. Elizabeth is handed down the set by Darcy himself — a surprise that silences the room — and the dance is a sparring match more than a courtesy. He is proud; she is playful; neither gives ground. The evening turns sour: Mrs Bennet talks too loudly about Bingley for Jane; Mary performs too long at the piano; and Lydia makes a spectacle. Darcy watches the family and calculates what association with them would cost. Bingley — who has danced twice with Jane and cannot see past his own happiness — notices none of this.

The ball crystallises two trajectories: Bingley moving toward Jane with uncomplicated warmth, Darcy moving toward Elizabeth against his own will and his own judgement. His coolness is not indifference — it is the rearguard action of a man who does not yet know he has already lost.

III · Mr Collins
Longbourn — proposals, Charlotte's bargain

Mr Collins arrives at Longbourn as a houseguest. He is the heir to the entail — a clergyman of profound self-satisfaction, patron-dropped names (Lady Catherine de Bourgh), and breathless deference to rank. His stated intention is to marry one of the Bennet daughters, which Mrs Bennet finds perfectly sensible and which the daughters find considerably less so.

He proposes to Elizabeth with a speech of extraordinary length and very little doubt about its outcome. She declines — firmly, repeatedly, and with increasing specificity. Mr Collins attributes each refusal to maidenly coyness, until Mr Bennet is asked to adjudicate and confirms with some satisfaction that Elizabeth means it. Within days, Mr Collins transfers his affections to Charlotte Lucas, Elizabeth's closest friend, and is accepted. The neighbourhood is briefly scandalized; Charlotte is entirely composed.

"I am not romantic, you know. I never was. I ask only a comfortable home."

— Charlotte Lucas, to Elizabeth, on accepting Mr Collins

Meanwhile, Bingley abruptly departs Netherfield. No farewell, no letter, no engagement. Jane receives a note from Caroline Bingley intimating that the party is settled in London for the winter — and gently suggesting that Bingley's attentions may tend in another direction entirely. Jane believes the best of everyone, so the wound takes longer to reach her than it should. Elizabeth is angrier on Jane's behalf than Jane allows herself to be.

Charlotte's pragmatism sits deliberately against Elizabeth's romantic expectations. Both women are making rational calculations given what the world offers them — they simply weigh the variables differently. Bingley's disappearance is the novel's first real blow, and the question of who engineered it will shape everything that follows.

IV · Wickham
Meryton — charm, lies, and a militia regiment

A militia regiment winters at Meryton, and the Bennet household — particularly Lydia and Kitty — is enchanted. Among the officers is George Wickham: handsome, easy, and possessed of a manner that opens drawing rooms and loosens confidences. He attaches himself to Elizabeth almost immediately, and over the course of a single evening walk in Meryton he tells her a long, smooth story about Darcy — how Darcy's late father loved Wickham like a son, left him a valuable living in his will, and how Darcy, out of jealousy, contrived to withhold it.

Elizabeth listens with gratifying outrage. Everything in the story confirms what she already thought of Darcy: arrogant, unscrupulous, quick to abuse power. Wickham is the story's victim and she becomes its indignant champion. She tells Jane; Jane suggests there may be fault on both sides. Elizabeth finds this generous but unlikely. The story circulates through Meryton and nobody questions it, because Wickham is charming and Darcy is cold and coldness looks very like guilt.

When Elizabeth next sees Darcy at a party, she meets his eye with an arch composure that tells him plainly she knows — or thinks she knows — everything. He meets her look and says nothing. She interprets his silence as shame. It is, but not for the reasons she imagines.

Wickham is the novel's most brilliant illustration of how prejudice is formed: Elizabeth hears one side of a story from a man who is beautiful to look at, and the story slots so perfectly into her existing view of Darcy that she never thinks to ask a question. Her intelligence, which she rightly prizes, becomes the very instrument of her manipulation.

V · Hunsford
Kent — Lady Catherine's court, a disastrous proposal

Elizabeth visits Charlotte at Hunsford parsonage in Kent. Charlotte, now Mrs Collins, manages her husband and her drawing room with quiet efficiency and no illusions. The neighbourhood is dominated by Rosings Park, seat of Lady Lady Catherine de Bourgh — Darcy's aunt — who dispenses opinions on all subjects with the confidence of someone who has never been contradicted. Elizabeth is unimpressed and says so, with just enough polish to be considered playful rather than rude.

Darcy and his cousin Colonel Fitzwilliam call at Rosings regularly. Darcy's visits to the parsonage grow more frequent than propriety strictly requires. During one private call, he proposes to Elizabeth — and he does it badly. He tells her he loves her despite the inferiority of her family, despite her connections, despite every argument against it. He seems to expect that this act of condescension will be received with grateful astonishment.

"In vain have I struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

— Mr Darcy, first proposal, Chapter 34

Elizabeth refuses him — and refuses him thoroughly. She accuses him of arrogance, of destroying Jane's happiness with Bingley, and of ruining Wickham's prospects. Darcy is shaken to silence. The next morning he leaves a letter at the parsonage door. The letter does not repeat the proposal. Instead it explains, point by point, the true history of Wickham — including his pursuit of Darcy's fifteen-year-old sister, Georgiana, and the attempted elopement for her fortune. Elizabeth reads the letter twice, then reads it again.

The letter is the pivot on which the whole novel turns. Every certainty Elizabeth has built about Darcy and Wickham must now be held up to the light and re-examined. The humiliation is double: she was wrong about Wickham, and she was wrong in a way that her own vanity made possible. Darcy, meanwhile, must reckon with the fact that his manner of proposing was itself a kind of insult.

VI · Pemberley
Derbyshire — Elizabeth sees Darcy truly

Travelling through Derbyshire with her aunt and uncle, the Gardiners, Elizabeth visits Pemberley on the assurance that its owner is away. The estate is magnificent — but more than that, it is tasteful. The housekeeper, Mrs Reynolds, speaks of Darcy with unstudied warmth: a good master, a kind brother, generous to every servant who has ever worked there. Elizabeth wanders through rooms of quiet, well-chosen elegance and finds her opinion of the man revising itself room by room.

Darcy arrives early and unexpectedly. The meeting on the lawn is the novel's most delicately managed scene: both characters flushed with surprise, both conscious of the last time they spoke, both changed. Darcy is warm, civil, and entirely without condescension. He asks to introduce his sister Georgiana, who is shy rather than proud. He invites the Gardiners to fish his river. He is, in every way, the man his housekeeper described.

"She had never seen him so desirous to please, so free from self-consequence, or unbending reserve."

— Narrator, on Elizabeth observing Darcy at Pemberley

Elizabeth is bewildered and a little disarmed. Before she can properly untangle what she feels, a letter arrives from Jane: Lydia has run away from Brighton with Wickham. There is no mention of marriage. The Gardiners cut the holiday short immediately, and Elizabeth — who has just, perhaps, allowed herself to wonder — must leave Pemberley without knowing if anything real might have grown here.

Pemberley functions as a second proposal by other means: the house, the grounds, the testimony of every person employed there, all make the case that Darcy could not make for himself in Kent. Elizabeth begins to understand that her first impression was a prejudice shaped by vanity — and that Wickham, the charming narrator of her mistake, is now the instrument of her family's catastrophe.

VII · Lydia's Ruin
Brighton and London — an elopement, a rescue in secret

Lydia has gone to Brighton as the guest of a militia officer's wife, against Mr Bennet's better judgement and with Elizabeth's express warning, which he did not act on. From Brighton she elopes with Wickham. No letter, no destination given. Wickham has debts all over Meryton; an elopement without marriage is ruin for the entire family. All five daughters lose their marriage prospects unless Lydia is found and made decent.

Mr Bennet goes to London to search; Mr Gardiner coordinates from his house in Gracechurch Street. Days pass. Then, abruptly, a letter arrives: the pair have been found, Wickham will marry Lydia, and Wickham's debts will be paid. Mr Bennet assumes his brother-in-law has paid the price and is appropriately mortified. Elizabeth assumes the same — until she learns, weeks later, that the negotiation was managed entirely by Darcy. He tracked Wickham through London, paid the debts, purchased the commission, and made the whole thing possible — then forbade Mrs Gardiner to mention it.

"If he had done it for Jane — but it was not for Jane's sake."

— Elizabeth, realising Darcy's motive, Chapter 52

Lydia — entirely unconscious of what has been arranged around her — arrives at Longbourn in triumph, full of wedding stories. She lets slip, carelessly, that Darcy was at the church. The secret unravels; Elizabeth writes to her aunt and receives the full account. She has, it becomes clear, allowed herself to love a man she had once dismissed — and he has spent a fortune saving a family he was under no obligation to save, and has told no one.

Darcy acts not from duty or calculation but because Elizabeth is the reason, and no other reason is offered or required. His silence about it — the deliberate withholding of credit — is the single most character-revealing action in the novel. Compare it to Wickham's confident public performance of virtue; the contrast is as sharp as the novel ever gets.

VIII · Two Proposals
Longbourn, autumn — Bingley returns, Darcy and Elizabeth

Bingley returns to Netherfield. He calls at Longbourn frequently; the calls grow longer; and one afternoon, walking in the garden with Jane, he proposes. Jane's happiness is complete, quiet, and entirely without drama. She does not know — and is never told — that Darcy spent months persuading Bingley to return, having accepted that his own interference with the match was wrong.

Lady Catherine de Bourgh sweeps into Longbourn uninvited, radiating authority and rumour. She has heard that Darcy intends to marry Elizabeth. She demands an assurance that no such engagement exists. Elizabeth refuses to give it. Lady Catherine leaves in fury. Darcy, learning of the encounter, takes it for exactly what it is: Elizabeth's implicit answer. He comes to Longbourn and, on a morning walk, tries again.

"You are too generous to trifle with me. If your feelings are still what they were last April, tell me so at once."

— Mr Darcy, second proposal, Chapter 58

This proposal is nothing like the first. He is uncertain; she is the one who must put him at ease. She tells him her feelings have entirely changed and she is grateful, deeply grateful. They walk two hours in the garden and tell each other, slowly, what the intervening months have looked like from the inside. Mr Bennet, when asked for consent, is genuinely astonished — and then, confirming in private that Elizabeth truly loves the man, gives it without reservation. Mrs Bennet, informed that her least favourite and most impertinent daughter is to marry a man worth ten thousand a year, discovers that she has always found him charming.

The second proposal works because both characters have changed. Darcy has shed his contempt for the Bennets and proved it by action; Elizabeth has shed her certainty and earned the humility to see clearly. The novel earns its resolution not by wish-fulfilment but by showing that pride and prejudice are curable — given time, self-knowledge, and the willingness to be wrong.

Why it matters
Legacy, themes, and the novel's enduring argument
The central argument
  • First impressions are formed by the observer's existing biases as much as by the person observed.
  • Social class shapes every relationship in ways that both characters and readers absorb without noticing.
  • Intelligence is not immunity to manipulation — it can be the instrument of it.
  • A good marriage requires mutual respect between equals, not romantic projection.

Pride

Darcy's pride is social — a habit of class — and Elizabeth's is personal — the pride of a sharp mind that does not like to be wrong. Austen is careful not to make one species virtuous and the other villainous: both must be dissolved before the novel can end.

Prejudice

Elizabeth forms her opinion of Darcy from a single overheard remark, confirms it through Wickham's fiction, and resists every revision until a letter forces the issue. Austen shows how prejudice is self-reinforcing: once established, it interprets all evidence in its own favour.

Marriage as economics

The Bennets' entail is not a background detail — it is the novel's engine. Every woman in the book must marry or be destitute. Charlotte's choice is not weakness; it is the clearest-eyed reading of the situation on offer. Austen never mocks her for it.

Irony as method

Austen's irony operates at every level: in narrator's voice, in dialogue, in plot. She never editoralises. The gap between what characters say and what they mean is the substance of almost every scene, and the reader is trusted to close that gap without guidance.

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

— Chapter 1

The opening sentence is not merely witty — it is a trap. Stated as universal law, it encodes the entire social logic the novel will spend 300 pages interrogating. The "truth" belongs to Mrs Bennet and her world; the irony belongs to Austen. Both coexist in the same sentence, and the reader must locate themselves between the two.

"I declare after all there is no enjoyment like reading!"

— Caroline Bingley, Chapter 11

Caroline says this while patently not reading, in order to seem cultured to Darcy. It is one of Austen's most economical ironic reversals, and it is misquoted ceaselessly by reading advocates who do not realise the speaker is an anti-reading hypocrite.

"You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

— Darcy, Chapter 34

The first proposal is beloved precisely because it fails. Darcy's declaration is a speech of competing motives — love and contempt intertwined — and it reveals more about his character in one page than the preceding thirty chapters. The failure is what makes the eventual success matter.

On misattribution

"I am not the only one who feels this way" is frequently attributed to Elizabeth Bennet or even to Austen herself. Neither said it. It is a modern internet coinage applied to Austen's name for authority. The actual Elizabeth Bennet tends to say things more carefully and with more irony than the internet gives her credit for.
