Sense and Sensibility

Jane Austen · 1811

Contents

Character Map

The Dashwood sisters, Ferrars, Willoughby and rivals

I · Displaced

Norland lost · Barton Cottage · The sisters established

The Dashwood women are turned out of their home. Mr Henry Dashwood has died, and by the laws of entail Norland Park passes to his son John from a first marriage. Fanny Dashwood makes the departure feel urgent. The opening chapter of the novel is a perfect comedy of progressive self-justification: John talks himself, in conversation with his wife, from promising the dispossessed family three thousand pounds to offering nothing at all.

The four Dashwood women move to Barton Cottage in Devonshire, on modest means. The cottage is small; the country is beautiful. The novel begins by establishing what the sisters have lost and what, in terms of character, they each bring to their reduced circumstances.

"The more I know of the world, the more I am convinced that I shall never see a man whom I can really love."

— Marianne Dashwood, Volume I
Elinor · Sense

Quiet, self-governing; already attached to Edward Ferrars but suppresses all visible feeling to spare her family anxiety.

Marianne · Sensibility

Open, passionate, romantic; would consider concealment of feeling a form of dishonesty, and says so, often.

The Novel’s Central Argument from the First Page

Austen sets up her structural opposition immediately: Elinor disciplines feeling; Marianne performs it. The novel will test both sisters, and its conclusion is not that one mode is right and the other wrong, but that both, taken to extremes, lead to suffering.

II · Barton and Willoughby

The romantic arrival · Music and poetry · A secret

Marianne sprains her ankle on the wet hillside. Out of nowhere rides Mr Willoughby: handsome, gallant, sweeping her up and carrying her home. He is everything Marianne had theorised about the ideal man. He loves the same poets, quotes what she quotes, feels what she feels. The whirlwind courtship lasts the whole of a Devonshire autumn. They are constantly together.

Elinor notices that there has been no formal proposal. Colonel Brandon, a quieter admirer of Marianne, is troubled by Willoughby in a way he does not explain. Then Willoughby leaves suddenly and coldly, without explanation, for London. Marianne is devastated in full view of everyone.

"It is not time or opportunity that is to determine intimacy; it is disposition alone."

— Marianne Dashwood, Chapter 12
Theme: The Dangers of Romantic Fantasy

Willoughby is the novel’s most unsettling figure because he is not simply a villain. He genuinely feels something for Marianne. He chooses money over her anyway. Austen understands this better than sentimentalism allows: feeling and behaviour are not the same thing, and the person who claims feeling while doing harm is worse, not better, than one who never felt at all.

III · Edward and Lucy

Elinor’s secret · Lucy Steele · The engagement revealed

Lucy Steele is pretty, insinuating, and ruthlessly intelligent in her own interest. She confides in Elinor as an act of deliberate cruelty dressed as intimacy: she has been secretly engaged to Edward Ferrars for four years. Elinor is the woman Edward clearly loves, and Lucy knows it perfectly well.

This is the chapter that establishes Elinor’s heroism. She must listen to Lucy describe her engagement with expressions of satisfaction. She must keep the secret. She must comfort her mother and sister through the Willoughby crisis, all while living with the knowledge that the man she loves is promised to another. She does it. She does not crack.

"Elinor sat down to her work, and the evening to her was not a sad one."

— Narrator, on Elinor concealing her pain, Volume II
Elinor as the Novel’s Moral Centre

The Lucy Steele episodes give Elinor her most complex test. She is not cold; she suffers intensely. What she possesses is not the absence of feeling but the ability to govern it in service of others. The novel asks: is this admirable self-sacrifice, or a suppression that costs the self too much? The answer it arrives at is honestly: both.

IV · London

The city visit · Willoughby’s cold letter · Brandon’s disclosure

The sisters travel to London. Marianne writes to Willoughby the moment they arrive and waits for his reply. At a party she sees him across the room and goes to him immediately. He is formal and cold, and introduces her to Miss Grey, his wealthy fiancee. Publicly, visibly, Marianne is destroyed.

His letter arrives: a masterwork of managed cruelty, polite and definitive, designed to deny he ever felt or promised anything. Colonel Brandon tells Elinor the truth about Willoughby: he had seduced and abandoned another young woman, leaving her destitute. Marianne grieves without restraint. Elinor holds everything together while receiving nothing for herself.

"Her tears fell without ceasing, and she was not ashamed of them."

— Narrator, on Marianne, Volume II
Elinor in London

Learns of Edward and Lucy from various sources; maintains composure; nurses Marianne; receives no comfort herself.

Marianne in London

Writes to Willoughby; is publicly rejected; receives a cold letter; grieves without restraint; becomes increasingly ill.

V · The Illness

Near death · Willoughby confesses · The transformation

Travelling back to Barton, the sisters stay at Cleveland. Marianne has been walking in wet grass, unprotected, in weather that does not forgive inattention. She falls dangerously ill. For several nights Elinor barely sleeps, nursing her, sending for their mother, convinced she may die.

Willoughby arrives at Cleveland, married, knowing Marianne is gravely ill, and intercepts Elinor. His confession is the most morally complex speech in the novel. He explains, he partially justifies, he mourns. He is genuinely sorry. He will be generally unhappy for the rest of his life. Austen delivers this without exonerating him: the sorrow is real, the responsibility is real, and both things are true at once.

"I felt that she was suffering, that she was the woman I had wronged, and that I deserved to be wretched."

— Willoughby to Elinor, Volume III

Marianne recovers. The illness is her crisis. Her romantic sensibility has literally made her self-destructive, and she emerges from the fever knowing this. She begins, quietly and genuinely, to see what Colonel Brandon has been all along.

The Illness as Moral Structure

Austen uses physical illness as a turning point without moralising it. Marianne’s near-death is not punishment, it is consequence. The novel does not say passionate feeling kills you. It says that feeling without self-governance, directed at an unworthy object, can make you too sick to live. The distinction is precise.

VI · Resolution

Edward freed · Two marriages · The novel’s verdict

Lucy Steele, with characteristic practicality, transfers her attentions to Robert Ferrars, Edward’s brother, the moment Edward is disinherited for refusing to break his engagement at his mother’s command. Edward, freed from his engagement and his conscience simultaneously, goes to Elinor.

His proposal scene is famously fumbling. He arrives; she assumes he is coming to announce his marriage to Lucy; he is there to ask her to marry him; she bursts into tears. Both of them are overwhelmed. The reader, who has watched Elinor hold herself perfectly composed for three volumes, finds this the most moving moment in the book precisely because the composure finally breaks.

"Elinor could sit it no longer. She almost ran out of the room, and as soon as the door was closed, burst into tears of joy."

— Narrator, Volume III

Marianne marries Colonel Brandon, not in passion but in respect that deepens into genuine love. Austen’s account of this is measured and honest: Marianne does not perform feeling she does not have, and she comes to have true feeling for him. The marriage she had fantasised about, wild and consuming, would not have made her happy. The marriage she makes will.

The Resolution: Integration, Not Defeat

Critics have argued that Marianne’s marriage to Brandon punishes her for romanticism. Austen is subtler: she does not suggest Marianne should have been Elinor. She suggests that sensibility without sense leaves the self unprotected. Both sisters end in marriages that require all of who they are. The title names the problem, not the solution.

Why It Matters

Legacy · Themes · Famous passages

Sense and Sensibility was Austen’s first published novel and her most structurally rigorous. Its double-protagonist design, Elinor and Marianne as complementary modes of being tested against the same world, has influenced the literature of interiority from George Eliot onwards. The novel refuses the easy verdict: neither sense nor sensibility is sufficient alone.

  • The title is a philosophical proposition contested in 1790s moral philosophy that Austen knew well; the novel tests both terms against lived experience rather than theory
  • Willoughby is literature’s most unsettling seducer because he genuinely feels; the novel makes feeling morally insufficient, even dangerous, as a category of self-defence
  • Elinor is the first fully realised portrait of the woman who manages everything for everyone while receiving nothing for herself, a recognition that has only deepened with time
  • The novel’s opening conversation, in which John Dashwood talks himself from generosity to nothing, remains a teaching text in how dramatic irony works at sentence level

Two Sisters as One Argument

Elinor and Marianne are not simple foils; they are a dialectic. The novel asks what happens when either mode is pushed to its extreme, then proposes integration as the answer.

Willoughby’s Defence

His late-novel speech is Austen’s most morally complex construction. He is remorseful, partly justified, and genuinely harmful. The novel holds all three without resolving them.

Economic Displacement

The Dashwoods are among Austen’s starkest examples of women’s economic vulnerability. The novel starts with a dispossession and never lets the reader forget what is at stake materially.

Feeling vs. Expression

Elinor feels deeply; Marianne expresses freely. The novel interrogates whether expression and feeling are the same thing and concludes, carefully, that they are not necessarily.

Three Famous Passages

"Elinor, this eldest daughter, whose advice was so effectual, possessed a strength of understanding and coolness of judgment... though her feelings were strong, she knew how to govern them."

The novel’s first description of Elinor, establishing from the outset that feeling and governing it are distinct capacities, both of which she possesses, and both of which the novel will test.

"It is not time or opportunity that is to determine intimacy; it is disposition alone."

Marianne defending her rapid attachment to Willoughby. She is right about disposition but wrong about his, and she cannot yet tell the difference. It is her most romantic statement and her most dangerous one.

"I wish as well as everybody else to be perfectly happy; but, like everybody else, it must be in my own way."

Edward Ferrars, in one of the novel’s most underrated lines. Its quiet insistence on individual modes of happiness anticipates the resolution: both sisters find happiness, but each in the form suited to who they actually are.

Three questions on themes and ideas

1. Austen titles the novel with two abstract nouns. What relationship between them does the story actually argue?

2. Willoughby genuinely loves Marianne but abandons her for an heiress. What does this argue about romantic feeling as a moral category?

3. Elinor suppresses her grief about Edward throughout the novel. Is this presented as admirable, excessive, or both?
