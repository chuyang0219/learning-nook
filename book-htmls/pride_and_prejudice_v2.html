The Bennet family and their world · Hertfordshire, c. 1813

I · A Single Man of Fortune

Hertfordshire · Netherfield arrives

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife. With this one sentence Austen turns the entire matrimonial economy of her world inside out — the observation belongs to the neighbourhood, not to reason, and what the neighbourhood believes is the engine of everything that follows.

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

— Opening sentence, Chapter 1

Mrs Bennet has heard that Netherfield Park, the great estate nearby, has been let at last. A young man of large fortune — Mr Bingley — has taken it, and in her mind he is already destined for one of her five daughters. Her husband, Mr Bennet, draws out his teasing to comic perfection before conceding that yes, he has already called on Bingley himself. The daughters know nothing of this yet. The comedy of the household is established in a page.

At the Meryton assembly ball, Bingley appears with his fashionable party: his sisters, their brother-in-law Mr Hurst, and his tall, grave friend Mr Darcy. Bingley dances every dance and singles out Jane Bennet as the most beautiful woman in the room. Darcy refuses to dance with anyone outside his own circle, declaring the other women not handsome enough to tempt him. He says this within earshot of Elizabeth Bennet, who is without a partner at the moment. She reports the slight to her friends with so much amusement that she has begun, already, to think ill of him — and he has begun, against his will, to notice her.

What happened

The Bennet family learns that Netherfield has been taken by the wealthy Mr Bingley. At the local assembly ball, Bingley charms everyone and dances repeatedly with Jane, while his friend Darcy insults Elizabeth by declaring her "not handsome enough to tempt" him.

Why it matters

The novel's central mechanism — pride masking attraction, and prejudice formed on thin evidence — is set in motion within its first public scene. Both Elizabeth and Darcy arrive at a wrong first impression that will take the entire novel to correct.

Characters

Mr Bennet — Witty, detached patriarch who finds his family's anxieties an endless source of quiet amusement.

Mrs Bennet — Nerves and matrimonial ambition in equal measure; her goal is daughters married, by whatever means.

Elizabeth Bennet — Sharp, warm, quick to laugh; her wit is her armour and, at first, her blind spot.

Mr Darcy — Rich, reserved, and accustomed to deference; his manner reads as contempt before the reader knows his character.

II · First Impressions

Balls, drawing rooms · Meryton and Longbourn

The neighbourhood visits at Netherfield multiply; Bingley's admiration for Jane becomes the talk of every dinner table. When Jane is caught in the rain on a visit and forced to stay several days, Elizabeth walks three miles through the mud to nurse her — arriving with muddy petticoat and bright eyes, to the disdain of the Bingley sisters and the covert admiration of Darcy. He catches himself noting the beauty in her face and quickly supposes himself imagining it.

Back in Meryton, a new regiment of militia has arrived, including the charming officer Mr Wickham. He attaches himself to Elizabeth on a walk and, within hours of their acquaintance, confides that Darcy has cheated him of a living promised by Darcy senior — making him a victim of Darcy's pride and cruelty. Elizabeth listens, is charmed by his manner, and believes every word. The contrast between Wickham's easy openness and Darcy's stiff reserve seems to confirm what she already thought.

"I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine."

— Elizabeth Bennet, on Darcy, Chapter 5

At the Netherfield ball — the social summit of the county season — Elizabeth is forced to dance with Darcy himself. Their conversation is a duel of wit and insinuation; each tests the other. Darcy is again discomfited by how much he wants to keep looking at her. The evening is ruined by the behaviour of the Bennet family: Mrs Bennet brays about Jane and Bingley; Mary plays the pianoforte too long; Mr Collins attaches himself to Darcy with obsequious speeches. Elizabeth burns with embarrassment. She cannot know that the evening has settled something in Darcy's mind — a resolve to save his friend from an unworthy alliance.

What happened

Elizabeth nurses Jane at Netherfield, where Darcy begins to admire her despite himself. She meets the plausible Wickham, whose tale of Darcy's injustice she readily believes. At the Netherfield ball, the Bennet family embarrasses themselves, prompting Darcy to resolve to separate Bingley from Jane.

Why it matters

Elizabeth's prejudice against Darcy is now confirmed (falsely) by Wickham's story — a tale she swallows because it flatters her existing opinion. Darcy acts on class pride to protect Bingley, setting the stage for both protagonists to cause each other real harm.

Characters

Wickham — A militia officer whose polished manner conceals a calculated dishonesty; he reads his audience perfectly and tells Elizabeth exactly what she wants to hear.

III · Proposals and Refusals

Longbourn · Hunsford · Kent

Bingley and his party abruptly quit Netherfield; letters from Bingley to Jane stop arriving. Caroline Bingley writes coolly to announce that the whole party has removed to London for the winter and hints that Bingley is quite taken with Darcy's sister. Jane reads it with composure; Elizabeth reads it with rage on her sister's behalf.

Into this dejection steps Mr Collins — the distant cousin who will inherit Longbourn on Mr Bennet's death. He arrives convinced that he must do the Bennet family a service by marrying one of them, and fixes upon Elizabeth as his choice. His proposal is a monument of pomposity: he cites the patronage of Lady Catherine, his modest but respectable income, and his certainty that Elizabeth cannot do better. She refuses him three times, with increasing clarity. He hears each refusal as feminine delicacy and presses on. Mrs Bennet is furious.

"You could not make me happy, and I am convinced that I am the last woman in the world who could make you so."

— Elizabeth Bennet, to Mr Collins, Chapter 19

Collins immediately transfers his attentions to Elizabeth's plain-spoken friend Charlotte Lucas, who accepts him within days. Elizabeth is quietly appalled — not at Charlotte, whom she understands, but at the world that makes such a bargain sensible. She visits Charlotte at Hunsford Parsonage in Kent and encounters the great house of Rosings, where Lady Catherine de Bourgh holds court and dispenses opinions without being asked.

What happened

Bingley leaves Hertfordshire, seemingly abandoning Jane. Mr Collins proposes to Elizabeth and is refused; he then proposes to Charlotte Lucas, who accepts. Elizabeth visits Hunsford and encounters Darcy's formidable aunt, Lady Catherine de Bourgh.

Why it matters

Charlotte's pragmatic choice frames the question Elizabeth faces throughout: can a woman of sense afford to hold out for feeling, or must she accept the economic terms of her world? Elizabeth refuses to compromise — but the cost of principle is made visible here.

Characters

Mr Collins — A clergyman whose self-satisfaction is impervious to irony; his proposal scene is Austen at her most comedically savage.

Charlotte Lucas — Sensible, practical, and clear-eyed about her options; she makes the rational choice, and Austen does not entirely blame her.

Lady Catherine de Bourgh — Patroness, tyrant, and walking embodiment of aristocratic presumption; she believes her rank entitles her to govern others' lives.

IV · Letters and Revelations

Hunsford · First proposal · The letter

Darcy has been visiting Lady Catherine's estate at Rosings at the same time as Elizabeth's stay. Their meetings have been stiff, argumentative, and — for Darcy — completely consuming. One evening he appears at the parsonage unannounced and, visibly agitated, tells Elizabeth that he has struggled against his feelings in vain. He loves her; he wants to marry her. He cannot speak of it without mentioning, extensively, the inferiority of her family and the condescension he is showing by the offer.

"In vain I have struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

— Mr Darcy, first proposal, Chapter 34

Elizabeth refuses him — not coldly but with passion. She tells him he has behaved ungentlemanly, that he separated Bingley from Jane without cause, and that he cheated Wickham of his future. Darcy is visibly wounded. The next morning he finds her walking and presses a letter into her hand; then he is gone.

The letter dismantles her. Darcy admits he discouraged Bingley — but explains, for the first time, what Wickham actually is: a gambler and a seducer who last year attempted to elope with Darcy's fifteen-year-old sister Georgiana for her thirty thousand pounds. Every accusation Elizabeth flung at Darcy collapses. The letter forces her to ask, for the first time, how much of her judgment was evidence and how much was vanity. "Till this moment," she admits to herself, "I never knew myself."

"Till this moment I never knew myself."

— Elizabeth Bennet, after reading Darcy's letter, Chapter 36
What happened

Darcy proposes to Elizabeth at Hunsford, coupling his declaration with condescending remarks about her family. She refuses him sharply. His letter the next morning reveals the truth about Wickham and his own motives, forcing Elizabeth to recognise the depth of her misjudgement.

Why it matters

This is the fulcrum of the novel. Both characters are simultaneously at their worst and on the verge of their best selves: Darcy's pride has made him offensive even in sincerity; Elizabeth's prejudice has made her unjust even in her cleverness. The letter begins the reformation of both.

V · Pemberley and Its Master

Derbyshire · The estate · Lydia's disgrace

Months later, touring Derbyshire with her aunt and uncle the Gardiners, Elizabeth visits Pemberley — Darcy's estate — confident he is absent. She sees the house and grounds for the first time: their beauty is natural, ungaudy, well-proportioned. The housekeeper praises Darcy without reservation — a kind master, a generous landlord, a devoted brother. Elizabeth finds herself revising her image of him a further time.

Darcy arrives unexpectedly, having come back early. He is changed: warm, attentive to the Gardiners, careful not to embarrass Elizabeth. He introduces his sister Georgiana — shy, gentle, nothing like the fortune-hunter Wickham described. The visit is tender and uncertain; both parties seem to want something neither can yet say plainly.

"She had never seen a place for which nature had done more, or where natural beauty had been so little counteracted by an awkward taste."

— On Pemberley, Chapter 43

Then the letter comes. Lydia has eloped with Wickham. They are somewhere in London — not married, possibly never intending to marry. The scandal would destroy the Bennet family. Elizabeth, white-faced, tells Darcy. He hears her out in silence, then leaves. She assumes she has now lost him entirely — that he will certainly not link himself to such a family.

What happened

Elizabeth visits Pemberley, sees the true Darcy through his estate and servants, and has a warm reunion with him. The visit is broken off by news that Lydia has eloped with Wickham — threatening the entire Bennet family with social ruin.

Why it matters

Pemberley is not just a house — it is the argument that Darcy is not what Elizabeth thought. The elopement crisis then demands that he choose between his pride and his love: whatever he does next will be the true measure of his character.

VI · Resolution

London · Longbourn · Second proposal

The scandal is resolved by Darcy — though Elizabeth does not learn this at once. He tracked Lydia and Wickham to London, found them, and paid Wickham's debts and a settlement sufficient to make the marriage happen. He did it in secret, asking only that his role not be disclosed. Mr Gardiner writes to say the matter is settled; the family assumes the Gardiners paid. But an accidental disclosure by Lydia reveals the truth, and Mrs Gardiner confirms it. Elizabeth is overwhelmed. He did it — she understands now — for her.

Lady Catherine arrives at Longbourn in a fury, having heard the rumour that Elizabeth may marry her nephew. She demands Elizabeth promise never to enter into such an engagement. Elizabeth, with magnificent composure, refuses: she is not engaged to Darcy, but she will not bind herself to stay that way.

"I am only resolved to act in that manner, which will, in my own opinion, constitute my happiness, without reference to you, or to any person so wholly unconnected with me."

— Elizabeth Bennet, to Lady Catherine, Chapter 56

Darcy returns to Hertfordshire with Bingley; Bingley proposes to Jane at once. Then Darcy and Elizabeth walk together, and he asks again — differently now, without pride, without conditions. She accepts. When she tells her father, he cannot believe it — she has seemed to dislike the man. "He is the man who, in every way — in ability and goodness — is most worthy of you," she tells him, and means it.

"You are too generous to trifle with me. If your feelings are still what they were last April, tell me so at once."

— Mr Darcy, second proposal, Chapter 58

Both Jane and Elizabeth marry well. Lydia and Wickham remove to the north and are gradually forgotten. Mr Collins carries on; Lady Catherine eventually relents. The novel closes quietly — not with a ball or a speech, but with the quiet satisfaction of two people who have changed enough to deserve each other.

What happened

It emerges that Darcy secretly financed Lydia and Wickham's marriage to protect the Bennets. Lady Catherine visits to forbid Elizabeth from marrying Darcy; Elizabeth refuses to comply. Darcy proposes a second time, now without pride; Elizabeth accepts. Bingley simultaneously proposes to Jane.

Why it matters

The resolution is earned by transformation: Darcy acts against his own class interest out of love; Elizabeth overcomes her prejudice through honesty with herself. Austen is careful that neither capitulates — each becomes more fully themselves in choosing the other.

Why it matters

Themes · Legacy · Famous passages

Published in 1813, Pride and Prejudice is simultaneously a comedy of manners, a social critique, and a psychological novel — a combination no previous English novel had achieved in quite this form. It has never gone out of print.

  • The novel operates on two registers at once: a surface of drawing-room comedy and an undertow of economic anxiety. Women in Regency England had no inheritance rights; marriage was survival.
  • Austen's irony is structurally built into the prose — every apparently neutral observation carries a judgement the reader must decode. This free indirect discourse was her greatest formal invention.
  • The novel argues that self-knowledge is a prerequisite for happiness — and that the obstacles to self-knowledge are vanity, habit, and the stories we tell about other people.
  • The hero and heroine reform each other without domination — a romantic model unusual in literature of any era.

Pride

Darcy's pride is not wickedness but a learned shield — his rank has accustomed him to deference. The novel asks what happens when the shield meets someone who will not defer.

Prejudice

Elizabeth's prejudice is the more dangerous flaw because it masquerades as cleverness. Her swift judgements feel like intelligence, which makes them harder to revise.

Marriage as Economics

Every marriage in the novel is an economic transaction as well as a personal one. Charlotte Lucas makes this explicit; Lydia makes it disastrous; Elizabeth makes it a matter of principle.

Free Indirect Style

Austen narrates from inside her characters' perspectives without using first person — a technique that lets irony and sympathy coexist on the same page.

Three most famous passages

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

— Chapter 1

The most famous opening sentence in English fiction — and a joke. The truth is the neighbourhood's delusion, not a fact of nature. In nine words Austen has established her ironic method and the society her characters must navigate.

"In vain I have struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed."

— Darcy, Chapter 34

Darcy's first proposal is the novel's great comic disaster: a declaration of love that reads like a complaint. Its importance is formal — it shows that even the most admirable character has yet to learn how to be humble.

"Till this moment I never knew myself."

— Elizabeth, Chapter 36

The moral pivot of the novel in six words. Elizabeth has been the reader's guide — witty, perceptive, reliable — and this sentence admits that the guide was lost. It is the moment the comedy deepens into something true.

One commonly misattributed line: "I declare after all there is no enjoyment like reading!" — spoken not by Austen but by the shallow Caroline Bingley, who says it to impress Darcy while not actually reading. Austen is making fun of the sentiment, not endorsing it.
